Why should you care?

V

1

The more we learn about things that are far away, the more we learn about things that are very close. By improving our understanding of the universe, we gain an improved understanding of how our solar system works, how our planet works, and how life itself works.

V

2

Humankind has no backup plan. We could be hit by a massive natural disaster — a solar storm, an untracked asteroid (like this one), a supervolcanic eruption. We could fall prey to a human-created catastrophe — nuclear war, climate change, water shortages. Some of these events could irrevocably change our way of life, while others could destroy not just humanity, but all organisms on Earth. It may not happen in our lifetime, our children’s lifetime, or their children's lifetime, but it will happen. To preserve life, we must journey to the stars.

V

3

There is a possibility that we may find living organisms elsewhere in the universe. This discovery would have massive repercussions for humanity. We would concretely know that our planet is not alone in harboring life and that, if there is life outside our solar system in one location, then there may be in others too. What we find may be big, but will likely be small. Finding such microorganisms would help us to trace life on Earth back to its roots, compare our biology to that of an extraterrestrial's, and answer many other questions about the origin of life in the universe. Answering these questions would lead to substantial progress in targeted treatments for diseases, therapies for genetic disorders, discovering the mechanisms by which many conditions and medications act on the body, and much more.

V

4

Space exploration offers us a multitude of opportunities to create new technologies that solve new problems. With history as our guide, we can see the numerous positive technologies that have arisen out of our desire to achieve the stars — scratch-resistant lenses, advancements in artificial limbs, lightweight firefighting equipment, freeze-dried food, solar cells for solar panels, systems to purify water, improved food safety, and devices that aid the body's natural functions (e.g. heart assists and insulin pumps). All of these technological advances emerged from our exploration of space near Earth. Imagine what new technologies we'll be offered the opportunity to create when we range out even farther into the cosmos.

V

5

Precious resources from our planet — water, rare metals, common metals, and even gases — are necessary to humankind's survival and progress as a species. These resources are not completely renewable and, within the next one hundred years, we will face problems with increasing costs and decreasing supplies. Fortunately, asteroids, comets, and other objects in our solar system are theorized to have vast quantities of these precious resources. Water has been observed on the dwarf planet Ceres in the asteroid belt, and scientists estimate that if even 25% of Ceres were composed of water, then it would contain more water than there is fresh water on Earth. Other asteroids may contain more rare metals than have been found on Earth since humanity began mining. We need not experience any crises due to lack of these resources on our planet, as massive quantities of them exist within our grasp. We have only to reach out and harvest them.

V

6

Humanity has ever been driven by the idea that we could make the future a little bit better — for ourselves, for our children, and for our children's children. By the idea that, if we dreamt it, we could create it. By the idea that, even if we didn't know everything, we could discover something. There has been no endeavor in human history that has made us hope more, innovate more, or wonder more than the exploration of space. To limit ourselves to only our planet would be to deny ourselves these basic ideas. We do not limit our children to their cradles; we encourage them to venture outwards — to explore, to wonder, and to grow. The Earth is our cradle, and it's time we crawled out of it.

Take Action

You can support the greatest scientific endeavor in human history — the exploration of space. We have selected several resources that will allow you to have an impact on the study and exploration of the cosmos. If this website has inspired you, we ask that you please get involved. Let your voice be heard and your efforts be worthwhile.

Contact Your Representative

Let your voice be heard by those who make budgetary decisions about our space science and exploration programs. Quickly and easily send a message to the president and members of congress to let them know that you believe space exploration is important to humanity's future. It may not seem like it, but every message sent and every voice heard does make a difference!

Want to go one step further? Call your representative's office and leave a message.

Planet Hunters

Discover and locate real planets by participating in this crowdsourced project created by Zooniverse. By marking dips in the brightness of stars (caused by objects moving between us and those stars, also known as transits) using Planet Hunters' intuitive web app, you will be providing analysis of actual data to scientists. If an analysis that you provide shows a transit, then that result will be flagged for observation by the Keck telescope in Hawaii!

Night Sky Network

Do you find your head tilting toward the stars when you're out at night? Do you think photos of galaxies and planets are awesome? You're not alone! Visit to find events, meet-ups, and astronomy clubs near you. Despite being small, local organizations can wield large influence.

No prior experience? Don't worry, they'll gladly help you out!

SETI@home

Put your excess computing power to work by participating in a massive distributed scientific experiment. All you need to do is install the free software, BOINC, and point it at the SETI@home URL — it does the rest itself! It will use your idle processing power to automatically analyze data captured by the Arecibo radio telescope, and search for anomalous patterns that could represent signals from other life or undiscovered cosmic phenomena.

Visit SETI@home

Planet Four

Are you interested in exploring a little closer to home? Then participate in Planet Four, a Zooniverse project created to improve our understanding of Mars' climate, which will prepare us to further explore the Red Planet, and possibly colonize it! The website will present high resolution images of the planets' surface and ask you to detect distinct land features that have been sculpted by Martian winds over millions of years. Your responses will be paired with hundreds of others from around the world to compile the first large-scale wind map of Mars!

Space Apps Challenge

For developers, designers, and engineers who want to put their talents to use, there is the annual International Space Apps Challenge, a global two-day hackathon focused only on space! Not just limited to apps, the challenge enables you to collaborate with space lovers from around the world by pairing hard data with your wildest ideas. Previous challenges have resulted in the creation of suborbital payloads, new methods for cooking in space, and a redesign of the Voyager probe's Golden Record! Sign up and see what happens!

Now that you know what's out there, you can help us get there.
